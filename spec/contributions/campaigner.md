## HUMAN TRUTH

In B2B import/export across Peru and LATAM, the buyer has always been the least informed person in the transaction.

The landed cost has always had five layers: product cost, freight, insurance, customs duties and taxes, last-mile delivery. Every quote the buyer has ever received compressed those five layers into a single number at the bottom of an invoice. The structure — the actual architecture of that number — was never shown. This was not accidental. Information asymmetry is how intermediaries retain leverage. The broker who controls the cost structure controls the relationship.

The buyer gets a number and has to take someone's word for it.

Mister does not give the buyer a number. Mister shows them the structure behind every number they will ever receive. That is a fundamentally different offer. It is not a faster way to get a quote. It is a way to arrive at any quote already knowing what it should look like.

### The cultural tension
In LATAM trade, information is the real currency. The person who understands cost structure has power over the person who does not. That knowledge lived for decades inside the heads of customs brokers, freight agents, and experienced trade specialists — and buyers remained dependent on those intermediaries precisely because the knowledge was not systematized or shared.

Mister makes that knowledge institutional, immediate, and available to the buyer before the first conversation with any supplier.

The word is precise. In trade, structure is technical: the cost structure, the document structure, the Incoterm responsibility structure, the corridor logistics structure. But it also carries weight in Spanish that "system" or "tool" does not. La estructura is what holds something up. Mister is what holds up an informed import decision.
